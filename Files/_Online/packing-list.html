Steyr 12M18 · Section One
Our truck, our list

Steyr 12M18
Expedition Camper

Jason, Mareike and Rocket. After 30,000+ km across Australia in different setups, and now preparing for full-time family life with our daughter in Europe, this is our refined, real-world packing list.

We focus on minimalism with redundancy — only keeping what we actually use. We try to live sustainably by reducing the use of plastic, synthetics and single-use items. This list continues to evolve as our daughter grows.

01 — Kitchen & Cooking

Cookware, tools, rituals

Per family of up to 4. Less is more — we keep only what we actually cook with.

  • Induction or gas cooktop — induction off solar is cleaner and safer. We use a double hob gas stove because it came with our setup.
  • 2 good knives — chef’s + paring. 2 quality knives beat 8 cheap ones.
  • Cast iron skillet — indestructible and doubles as a baking dish.
  • 1 medium pot + 1 large pot — covers 95% of meals.
  • 4× plates + 4× bowls — lightweight, unbreakable. One per person is enough.
  • 4× mugs + cutlery — extra teaspoons. We use them constantly.
  • Cutting board, spatula, tongs, whisk — wooden or metallic if possible.
  • One stainless or wooden mixing bowl — for salads. More sustainable than collapsible plastic.

Section One · continued
01 — Kitchen & Cooking (cont.)

Coffee, fridge,
pantry, cleanup

  • Coffee setup — Moka Pot + USB-C grinder. Whatever your ritual is, protect it.
  • Small electric kettle — electric if you have the power, otherwise boil in a pot.
  • Lighter + matches — we always have a couple of each.
  • Airtight food storage — glass or stainless steel preferred over plastic.
  • Biodegradable soap, brush, tea towels — avoid sponges. They go mouldy fast.
  • Trash + compost bag — ours is a 20L bin. Small enough to fit most bin slots, big enough for daily use.
  • Spices & oil — salt, pepper, cinnamon, cumin, paprika, oregano, Italian herbs, turmeric...
12V fridge / freezer We run a 90L fridge + a separate 32L freezer. Upright fridges never give enough freezer space — a dedicated freezer is a game-changer.
Pantry staples Tinned: tomatoes, tuna, beans, lentils, corn. Dried: pasta, oats, rice, flour, couscous, soy protein, chia. Fresh: onion, garlic, eggs, wraps. The backbone of fast meals anywhere.
What we removed

Too many pots, plates and bowls. A big rice cooker. Excessive gadgets. We learned that one well-loved tool replaces three half-used ones — and the cast iron skillet does more than any non-stick we’ve ever owned.

Section Two
02 — Clothing & Personal Items

A small wardrobe
that works across climates

Per person. Functional, layered, sustainable materials — without bulk.

Tops & layers

  • 6 versatile tops — easily matched. Black is best; white isn’t ideal if you wash all colours together.
  • 2 merino mid-layers — one merino does the work of three synthetics.
  • 1 heavy outer — down jacket for cold nights.
  • 1 waterproof jacket — properly waterproof, not “showerproof”.

Bottoms & smart

  • 3 jeans/pants + 3–4 shorts
  • 1 versatile smart pair — for cities and border crossings.

Base layers

  • 10 merino / cotton / bamboo underwear — merino is best but the most expensive.
  • 8 pairs of merino socks

Footwear & accessories

  • 1 pair running shoes — we hike in these too.
  • 1 pair sturdy walking shoes — our Blundstones go on almost daily.
  • 1 pair sandals
  • Swim gear
  • Sun hat + warm beanie — pack for both climates, always.
  • Quality sunglasses

For the baby

  • Baby clothes — size up. Pack 2 sizes ahead; they grow fast.
  • Baby blankets + lightweight sleeping bag

Laundry

  • Dirty-laundry bag, travel detergent, Scrubba Bag — the Scrubba was a game-changer in Australia. Extends time between laundromat trips.

Section Three
03 — Toiletries & First Aid

Solid bars,
real first-aid

Toiletries

  • Solid shampoo + conditioner bars — no spill risk, less waste, last longer.
  • Solid soap bar — same reasons.
  • Bamboo toothbrushes + toothpaste
  • Natural deodorant
  • Sunscreen (high SPF)
  • Lip balm with SPF — often forgotten, always needed.
  • Menstrual cup / reusable pads
  • Tweezers + nail kit
  • Baby toiletries — gentle wash, nappy cream, natural moisturiser.

FIRST AID KIT (CONDENSED VERSION)

  • Comprehensive first aid kit — build your own. Pre-made ones are usually thin.
  • Prescription medications + surplus — always carry more than you think.
  • Pain relief — paracetamol + ibuprofen
  • Antihistamines — tablets + cream
  • Rehydration sachets — more useful than expected with a baby.
  • Wound care — bandages, steri-strips, antiseptic, sterile gauze.
  • Tick removal tool — especially with a dog.
  • SAM splint — lightweight, immobilises any limb.
  • Emergency foil blanket — weighs nothing, worth everything.
  • Digital thermometer — essential with a baby.
  • Infant paracetamol + saline drops

Section Four
04 — Baby Gear

New-parent
overlanding edition

Prioritise multi-use and compact. Less is better when space is limited.

  • Travel bassinet or bedside sleeper — plan for this in the interior design. The space has to adapt as the baby grows.
  • Car seat, properly mounted — check approval requirements for every country you enter.
  • Structured carrier + wrap/sling — two types for different ages. We bought two second-hand, one each, so neither of us has to readjust every time.
  • Portable nappy bag
  • Cloth nappy system + wet bags — practical with solar-powered washing, reduces waste and ongoing cost.
  • Baby clothes + 4+ muslin cloths — the most versatile baby item: blanket, sun shade, cover, change mat.
  • Bottles, steriliser, breast pump — if needed.
  • Breastfeeding support — pillow, nipple cream, breast pads.
  • Baby first aid — thermometer, infant paracetamol, saline, rehydration sachets.
  • Baby nail scissors + file
  • Dummies + clip attachments — they disappear. Have spares.
  • Soft books, rattles, teething items — keep minimal.
  • Portable high chair or booster seat
  • Picnic blanket / play mat — a good picnic blanket works as a play mat and stays useful long after the baby stage.

Section Five
05 — Dog Gear

Rocket’s kit

  • Dog bed/blanket — their own space in the truck keeps them settled.
  • Food and water bowls
  • Spill-proof water bowl for the cab
  • Dry food storage + treats — we buy in bulk. Saves money and means we only restock 2–3 times a year.
  • Harness, leash, long line — long lead for campsites where you want them to roam freely.
  • Dog first aid kit — tick remover, paw balm, bandages, gauze, antiseptic, vet wrap.
  • Poop bags + portable waste container — lots of poop bags.
  • Brush / grooming tools
  • Favourite toys (2–3)
  • Dog towel — we have at least two, for when he’s wet and dirty.
  • Flea and tick prevention — essential in Australia and the tropics. We have an annual supply of his monthly pills.
  • Pet passport + vaccination records — essential for border crossings.
  • FAVN rabies titre paperwork — required for  several countries.
Optional

Cooling mat for the cab — for hot climates. Soaked, sat on, dry by lunchtime.

Section Six & Seven
06 — Recovery & Off-Road

When the road
runs out

  • Synthetic rope
  • Soft shackles + tree-trunk protector
  • Snatch straps + kinetic rope — properly rated, not a cheap tow rope.
  • Traction boards — aluminium, MaxTrax or similar. Sand, mud, snow — changes everything.
  • Shovel
  • Tyre repair kit + 12V compressor
  • Hi-Lift jack + base plate — a standard jack is useless in soft ground.
  • 2 full-size spare tyres — one is a necessity. Two is peace of mind. (Our second is currently off the truck. Shame on us.)
07 — Tools & Vehicle Maintenance

Adjust to your specific vehicle

  • Comprehensive tool kit — make sure you know what you need for your vehicle.
  • Multimeter + wire, fuses, connectors — electrical gremlins will happen.
  • Vehicle-specific spares — filters, belts, hoses, fuses.
  • WD-40, duct tape, zip ties, wire
  • Tyre pressure gauge + deflator
  • Engine oil + coolant
  • Torque wrench
  • Head torch — for working under the truck at night.
  • Work gloves — dirty jobs, recovery, firewood.
  • Spare bolts, screws, ratchet straps

Section Eight
08 — Living & Comfort

Sleep well.
Sit well. Eat well.

SLEEPING & SITTING

  • Quality sleeping bags / duvet setup — we use a 3-in-1 duvet for all seasons. If that’s still not warm enough, a throw goes on top.
  • 2× fitted sheets — right size for your mattress. One in use, one in the wash.
  • Good mattress or topper
  • 2× lightweight camp chairs — you use these every single day.
  • Compact folding table — eating off your lap gets old fast.

Outside & evenings

  • Awning — shade and rain protection.
  • Lanterns + string lights — makes camp feel like home. Underrated.
  • Outdoor mat — keeps the truck floor clean.
  • Fire kit — lighter, waterproof matches, firestarter.
  • Water filter or tablets — backup when the tank runs low.
  • Jerry cans — water + fuel. Capacity depends on your range needs.
  • Books, cards, games — our favourites: Backgammon, Monopoly Deal, Uno, a normal deck. Two Kindles — less room than physical books. Not the same feeling, but a fair compromise.
Optional

Blackout window covers — essential in cities and for babies; doubles as privacy

Outdoor shower. Outdoor kitchen / BBQ.

Section Nine & Ten
09 — Safety & Emergency

The non-negotiables

  • Fire extinguishers — cab + living area. Mounted and accessible at all times.
  • Carbon monoxide + smoke detectors
  • Satellite communicator — Garmin inReach or similar. Non-negotiable with a baby in remote areas. Two-way messaging from anywhere.
  • USB-C headlamps × 2 — one per person, always.
  • Emergency cash — cards fail. Cash is universal.
10 — Electronics & Power

Starlink, solar, storage

  • Electric setup that suits your needs — mounted solar, portable panels, battery bank, USB outlets. We may write a separate guide on sizing and wiring.
  • Laptop + chargers
  • Starlink — game-changer for remote connectivity and working on the road.
  • Camera kit + drone
  • External hard drives — for footage backup. Keep one copy off-site if you can.
  • Multi-country power adapter set

Section Eleven & Twelve
11 — Documents & Admin

Paper that crosses borders

  • Passports (including baby) — check validity. Many countries require 6+ months remaining.
  • Vehicle documents + Carnet de Passage — originals + copies.
  • International driving permits — both drivers.
  • Birth certificate + certified copies — for the baby.
  • Travel insurance policy — premium tier with medivac, not basic.
  • Vaccination records — family and Rocket.
  • Emergency cash + digital backups — cloud + USB. Cards fail.
  • Printed emergency contacts — do not rely only on your phone.
12 — Navigation

Offline-first, phone-second

  • Offline maps — OsmAnd or Maps.me. Download before leaving signal range.
  • Dedicated GPS device — phone dies. GPS doesn’t.
  • Compass — irreplaceable when electronics fail.
  • Local SIM cards — buy on arrival in each country.
  • Satellite communicator — Garmin inReach or similar. Two-way messaging from anywhere. (See Safety.)
Optional

Paper road atlas — when data fails, paper works.

Section Thirteen & Fourteen
13 — Seasonal Variations

Hot & wet.
Cold & rainy.

Australia — hot / wet

  • Extra insect protection, shade solutions, water storage
  • Fly nets — there are flies absolutely everywhere
  • Light clothing + quick-dry items

Europe — cold / rainy / winter

  • Heavy insulation layers, good waterproofing
  • Snow chains or socks (if needed)
  • More heating solutions + a dehumidifier
14 — Final Tips

What we’d tell ourselves before we left

Weight is everything. Prioritise multi-purpose items. Test everything on short trips first.

With a baby, focus on quick setup and teardown. Every minute counts at camp.

Weight and space management is always important when camping. We regularly do “purge rounds” and remove items we haven’t used in months. This list is what works for us — use it as a starting point and adapt it to your own truck, climate, and family. Send us your thoughts, recommendations and ideas — we’re always keen to learn.

Safe travels,
Jason, Mareike, our daughter and Rocket

Before you copy this list

This is what works for our truck, our daughter, our dog, and the routes we’ve actually been on. It isn’t a recommendation, an endorsement, or a strict packing list for your family. Vehicle laws, border rules, baby gear standards, and dog import requirements change country to country and year to year. Use this as a starting point, not a finish line — and weigh the truck before you leave.
